# SwarmSPX — ML Research Plan
**Author**: ML Research (ex-DeepMind, RL on financial time series)
**Date**: 2026-04-27
**Audience**: Project owner, eng lead, future quant hire
**Mandate**: What does it take to turn a 24-LLM debating swarm into something hedge funds and quants haven't seen?

---

## Thesis

The LLM swarm is the *user interface* and the *narrative engine*. It is **not** the alpha source. Alpha for SPX 0DTE comes from microstructure features, realized-volatility geometry, regime-conditional probability calibration, and conformal-bounded position sizing — all of which are classical ML problems that 14B-parameter local LLMs cannot solve. The right architecture is a **hybrid**: a fast classical-ML predictor (boosting + temporal transformer + conformal wrapper) is the *brain*; the LLM swarm is the *cortex* — it parses unstructured text (FOMC, earnings, news), generates regime hypotheses, and produces auditable trade rationales. Treat them as different organs and stop asking the LLMs to predict prices.

The single biggest gap right now: there is no probability-calibrated, P&L-aware target signal. The OutcomeTracker measures equity moves rather than option P&L (per `00-SUMMARY.md` issue #1), so even if the LLM consensus had alpha, the feedback loop cannot detect it. Fix that, then bolt classical ML on top, then make the LLMs work on text — in that order.

## 1. The right ML stack for 0DTE SPX

### 1.1 Features — what is signal vs. noise

For 0DTE SPX, the half-life of any predictive feature is in minutes, sometimes seconds. The feature set must reflect this.

**High signal (microstructure, sub-minute):**
- **Order-book imbalance** at top-of-book and at L5 — `(B − A) / (B + A)` on resting size; refresh per tick. Predictive at 30s–5min horizons in equity-index futures (Cont, Kukanov, Stoikov 2014).
- **Signed volume / Lee-Ready trade direction** — bid-side volume minus ask-side volume over rolling windows. Use `polygon`/Schwab L1 ticks; fall back to tick rule if no L2.
- **VPIN (Volume-synchronized Probability of Informed Trading)** — Easley, López de Prado, O'Hara (2012). Detects toxicity in order flow; jumps before flash dislocations. Implementable on volume buckets, not time bars. Works on /ES too.
- **Realized kernel and bipower variation** — Barndorff-Nielsen et al.; jump-robust integrated variance. Better than 5-min realized vol for 0DTE risk.
- **Hawkes-process intensity on trade arrivals** — captures self-exciting clustering before vol spikes. Two-line fit with `tick` library.

**Mid signal (cross-asset, 1–30 min):**
- **DXY, TLT, USO** — dollar/bonds/oil. SPX has stable conditional correlation with TLT in risk-off; correlation regime-switches.
- **VIX term-structure slope** — VIX9D / VIX, VIX / VIX3M, VVIX, SKEW. Front-end inversion is the single most reliable "buy puts" signal.
- **Sector dispersion** — S&P sector ETF rolling correlation; collapsing dispersion precedes volatility events.
- **/ES vs SPX cash basis** — the futures premium/discount predicts direction better than either alone in opening 30 minutes.

**Slow signal (regime, hours–days):**
- **HMM state on (return, realized-vol, dispersion)** — discrete regime label. Reasonable: 4 states (low-vol-up, low-vol-down, high-vol-up, high-vol-down).
- **GARCH(1,1) conditional variance** — feed as feature, not as the target.
- **Macro surprise indices** — Citi MSI, ISM, NFP surprise. Slow but conditioning matters.

**Low signal / noise (what to drop):**
- Raw OHLCV without normalization. Models memorize price level.
- Plain RSI/MACD on minute bars. The crowd uses these; alpha is largely arbed away.
- Twitter/Reddit sentiment as a primary feature. Useful only as a regime filter (panic/euphoria flag), not a continuous predictor.
- "News count" without semantic parsing. The LLM should turn news into structured features (see §3); the count itself is mostly noise.

**Feature hygiene (de Prado, Advances in Financial ML, 2018):**
- Use **dollar bars** or **volume bars**, not time bars, for microstructure features. Time bars are the wrong sampling under heteroscedasticity.
- Apply **fractional differentiation** to preserve memory while passing stationarity tests (chapter 5). Standard `diff(log(P))` destroys long-memory structure that GARCH/HMM models exploit.

### 1.2 Target — what to predict

The target choice is more important than the architecture. Recommended **multi-task** head:

1. **Direction** at 30-min horizon (binary: SPX up/down by ≥ X bps where X is regime-conditional).
2. **Realized vol** over next 30 min (regression, log-RV target).
3. **Probability of touch** for the nearest OTM strike (binary; this is what 0DTE actually pays).
4. **Maximum favorable excursion (MFE) and maximum adverse excursion (MAE)** for an option held T minutes — for stop/target placement.

Why multi-task: shared lower-trunk representations are a free regularizer, and the four heads reflect the actual decisions a trader makes (entry, sizing, stop, target). Reference: Caruana 1997; for finance specifically, Lim et al. 2021 (Temporal Fusion Transformer multi-horizon).

**Critical fix for SwarmSPX**: change `OutcomeTracker` to label every signal with the **option-leg P&L**, not the SPX move. Until that is fixed every model trained on the resolved labels will learn the wrong objective.

### 1.3 Architecture — when does deep beat boosting?

Empirical truth in 2026:
- **Tabular features with weak temporal structure → XGBoost/LightGBM/CatBoost wins.** Gu, Kelly, Xiu (2020, RFS, "Empirical Asset Pricing via Machine Learning") found gradient-boosted trees and shallow networks tie or beat deeper architectures on monthly cross-sectional returns.
- **Strong temporal structure with regime shifts → temporal transformers win, but only with enough data.** Temporal Fusion Transformer (Lim et al. 2021) and PatchTST (Nie et al. 2023) beat boosting on minute-level futures when training set > ~5M samples.
- **Long-horizon, sparse-event prediction → state space (Mamba, S4) is the new SOTA.** Gu & Dao 2023. Linear-time recurrence handles the 10K-step contexts that 0DTE needs (1-second bars × 6.5 hour session).
- **N-BEATS / NHITS** still competitive for univariate forecasting; less useful for multivariate microstructure.

**Concrete recommendation for SwarmSPX:**
1. **Workhorse model**: LightGBM with 200 quantile bins, monotonic constraints on volatility features (`monotone_constraints`), early stopping. This will be the production model. Sub-millisecond inference per row on a 4090's CPU side.
2. **Challenger**: PatchTST on 30-min lookback of microstructure features, distilled to ~5M params for sub-100ms inference.
3. **Ensemble**: stacked blender — LGBM + PatchTST + a tiny MLP on macro features → logistic meta-learner with isotonic calibration.
4. **Do not** use a vanilla LSTM on raw price. It will look great in-sample, fail walk-forward, and you will spend three months debugging it.

### 1.4 Probability calibration — non-negotiable for sizing

A model that outputs 0.7 must be right 70% of the time on holdout, conditional on it saying 0.7. Almost no off-the-shelf model does this without calibration.

- **Platt scaling**: simple, parametric (sigmoid). Works when the miscalibration is monotone.
- **Isotonic regression**: nonparametric, monotone. Better when miscalibration is curved. Default choice.
- **Beta calibration** (Kull, Silva Filho, Flach 2017): sometimes better than isotonic with limited data.
- **Conformal prediction** (see §5) — gives finite-sample, distribution-free coverage guarantees.

The Kelly fraction — the only mathematically optimal sizing rule under log utility — is `f* = (p·b − q) / b`. If your `p` is off by 10%, your optimal fraction can be off by 100%. Calibration is the single highest-leverage post-processing step in the entire pipeline.

### 1.5 Ensembling — boring but it works

- **Bagging** (random subsets of training data, average predictions) reduces variance. ~0.5–1% AUC bump for free.
- **Boosting** (sequentially fit residuals) reduces bias. Already in LGBM/XGBoost.
- **Stacking** (meta-learner on top of base learners' outputs) reduces both. Use a **purged k-fold CV** (de Prado ch. 7) on the base layer to avoid label leakage into the meta-learner.

Ensemble of {LGBM, PatchTST, HMM-state classifier, XGBoost on engineered features} → meta-learner is the right shape. The 24-LLM swarm becomes a 25th base learner, weighted by its calibration on out-of-sample data, not by its ELO score on directional accuracy alone.

## 2. Reinforcement learning — useful or vanity?

**Honest answer: vanity in 80% of trading-RL use cases. Useful in 20%, but only with proper care.**

What does not work:
- Vanilla DQN/PPO on (price, vol) → action ∈ {long, short, flat}. The reward signal is too sparse, the episode dynamics nonstationary, and the policy overfits to the in-sample regime. Every published "RL beats buy-and-hold" paper that does not cross-validate over regimes is a backtest artifact.
- Rainbow DQN, A2C, etc. on minute bars. Same.
- Continuous-action sizing with Soft Actor-Critic on raw return as reward. The Bellman equation under transaction costs and slippage is **not** what the agent learns; the simulator must be accurate to within bps for the policy to transfer.

What does work:
- **RL for stop/target/exit policies given an entry signal from a separate classifier**. The classifier handles direction; RL handles "given I am long this 0DTE call, when should I roll, take profit, or cut?" Reward = realized after-cost option P&L minus a slippage penalty. This is the right factorization (Kolm, Tütüncü, Fabozzi 2014; more recently Théate & Ernst 2021).
- **Risk-sensitive RL** with CVaR-constrained rewards (Tamar, Glassner, Mannor 2015). Critical for 0DTE, where the median day is a slow grind and the bad days are catastrophic.
- **Realistic execution simulator** is the biggest leverage point. Use historical L2 order book replays with queue-position modeling, not a fill-at-mid simulator. Almgren–Chriss 2001 for static; Cartea, Jaimungal, Penalva 2015 for the modern Hamilton–Jacobi–Bellman formulation.

**SwarmSPX recommendation**: do not build RL until §1 ships and runs profitably for 90 days. Then build a single, narrow RL agent: input = (entry features, current option Greeks, time-of-day, realized vol, P&L state), action = ∈ {hold, partial-close-25%, partial-close-50%, full-close, roll-strike}, reward = next-step after-cost P&L change with a time-decay penalty. PPO with a recurrent encoder. Keep the action space small, the simulator honest, and the training horizon short.

## 3. Modern LLM uses that aren't direction prediction

This is where SwarmSPX has the most underused capacity. A 14B local model is excellent at structured extraction and regime narration; it is mediocre at numeric prediction.

**3.1 FOMC / earnings / macro release parsing → structured features.**
Fine-tune (or even few-shot prompt) the local Phi-4 14B on labeled FOMC statements to output:
- `hawk_dove_score ∈ [-1, 1]`
- `forward_guidance_change ∈ {hawkish, dovish, neutral, ambiguous}`
- `policy_path_pivot_probability ∈ [0, 1]`
- `surprise_magnitude ∈ {none, small, large}` vs. consensus
- `sentiment_delta` vs. previous statement (cosine distance on extracted policy vector)

These become numeric features into the §1 model. Goyal & Welch (2008) and Bybee, Kelly, Manela, Xiu (2024, "The Structure of Economic News") show structured text features add measurable predictive power that raw embeddings do not.

**3.2 Multimodal: chart-image embeddings + numerical features.**
Use a vision-language model (Llama 3.2 11B Vision or LLaVA on 4090) to embed candlestick patterns. Concatenate with engineered numeric features. Feeds into the meta-learner. This sounds gimmicky and would be — except that visual pattern embeddings encode prior-bar geometry that humans use and most quant models throw away. Worth a 1-week spike.

**3.3 News-to-vol forecast (lead-time arbitrage).**
Subscribe to a low-latency news feed (Benzinga Pro, Newsfilter.io, or scrape). The local LLM classifies headline → (vol-impact ∈ {none, low, high}, direction ∈ {risk-on, risk-off, neutral}, time-to-impact ∈ {seconds, minutes, hours}). Do this at <1s end-to-end. Feed a vol-spike alert into the strategy selector. The market typically reacts in 1–5s; a sub-second classifier is real lead time, not look-ahead bias. Tetlock (2007, JF) is the foundational reference; the modern version is real-time and uses local models.

**3.4 Synthetic adversarial trade generation.**
Use the LLM swarm to generate **plausible-but-adversarial** market scenarios (FOMC surprise, flash crash, gap-up open) and stress-test the strategy. Not for training the predictor (synthetic data poisons it) — for stress-testing the position sizer.

**3.5 In-context regime classification with structured rationale.**
Give the LLM the last 2 hours of bars, VIX state, news headlines, and ask: "Classify regime ∈ {trending up, trending down, mean-reverting, breakout, blowoff}. Cite three pieces of evidence." Use the regime label to switch which classical model is in charge (regime-conditional ensemble). LLMs are good at this; classifiers struggle without massive labeled data.

**3.6 Fine-tuning a 7B model on 10 years of FOMC + jobs reports labeled with subsequent SPX vol.**
LoRA-fine-tune Llama 3.1 8B on (statement_text, next_60min_realized_vol). Predicts vol from text alone. Bybee/Kelly-style. Approx 10K labeled examples is enough for LoRA. This produces a useful feature for §1 with one weekend of GPU time on the 4090.

## 4. Self-play / GAN-style swarm idea

**Premise**: a "bull swarm" of 12 agents and a "bear swarm" of 12 agents, scored against each other in a simulator that replays historical SPX with hidden labels, with periodic distillation of the winning swarm's policy into the loser's prompts.

**Could this work? Conditionally yes, with two big caveats.**

**What works in this design:**
- The discriminator side (a calibrated classifier judging whether a price path is "real" given features) is trainable on real data. This is essentially a Wasserstein GAN with a feature-matching discriminator (Goodfellow 2014; Yoon et al. 2019 TimeGAN for the financial-specific version).
- Tournament-style fitness selection for prompt mutations works well for LLM agents — see the "Promptbreeder" line of research (Fernando et al. 2023).
- The competition forces calibration: an agent that says BULL with 95% confidence and is wrong 30% of the time loses to one that says BULL with 60% and is right 60%.

**Failure modes:**
- **Mode collapse**. Both swarms converge to the same prediction (whatever the prior is). GAN literature is full of this; financial GANs are worse because the data is non-stationary (Wiese, Knobloch, Korn, Kretschmer 2020).
- **Reward hacking via self-confirmation**. If a swarm controls both the simulated market and the trades, it learns to manipulate the simulated price rather than predict the real one. Solution: use **frozen historical replays**, not generated-by-self markets.
- **Distillation collapse**. If you copy the winner's prompts into the loser, both swarms converge in 5–10 generations and diversity collapses. Need a forced minimum-diversity constraint (e.g., reject distillation if cosine similarity of system prompts exceeds 0.9).
- **The base LLM quality dominates**. Self-play improves *coordination* and *calibration* but cannot exceed the base model's reasoning ceiling. Local 14B has a hard ceiling.

**SwarmSPX-specific recommendation:**
Build it, but as a **calibration trainer**, not a predictor. Run the bull-vs-bear tournament for 6 months of historical data, using each round's realized outcome to adjust each agent's persona prompt (small mutation). Score by **Brier score** (calibration loss), not directional accuracy. The artifact is a set of well-calibrated agents whose vote *probabilities* are meaningful inputs to §1's stack.

## 5. Conformal prediction for trading — the hidden gem

Why nobody uses it: most quants haven't read Vovk, Gammerman, Shafer 2005, or Angelopoulos & Bates 2023's tutorial. They should.

**What it gives you**: distribution-free, finite-sample guarantee that your predicted probability is right with coverage 1−α, *no matter what model you used*. If you say "70% chance of a 50-cent option doubling in 30 min", conformal prediction can wrap that and give you a *prediction interval* that covers the true probability at 90% confidence by construction.

**Why this matters for SwarmSPX**:
1. **Kelly sizing is brittle.** With Kelly, a 5% miscalibration in p makes a 30% difference in optimal size. Conformal-bounded Kelly (Cella & Ryan 2020) uses the lower bound of the conformal interval as the input to Kelly, which removes the catastrophic-overbet failure mode.
2. **Adaptive to non-stationarity.** Standard calibration breaks under regime shifts. Conformal can use a sliding window of recent residuals (CP-OGD, Gibbs & Candès 2021) and stay valid as markets change.
3. **Interpretable for the trader.** Instead of "model says 0.7", you get "model says 0.7, conformal interval [0.55, 0.85]". The trader (or the strategy selector) can size on the lower bound.

**Implementation**: 30 lines with `mapie` or `nonconformist`. Wrap any classifier. Use **adaptive prediction sets** (Romano et al. 2020) for classification; **conformalized quantile regression** (Romano, Patterson, Candès 2019) for vol forecasting. This is the highest-leverage 1-week project in the entire plan.

## 6. The actual training pipeline

The pipeline matters more than the model. Here is the right shape.

```
[Schwab L1/L2 + /ES + Tradier options + Newsfilter]
   │
   ▼
[Tick store] ── dollar bars / volume bars
   │
   ▼
[Feature factory]
   ├── microstructure (OBI, VPIN, signed vol, RK, BPV)
   ├── cross-asset (DXY, TLT, USO, sector dispersion)
   ├── regime (HMM state, GARCH cond. var, vol term structure)
   ├── text (LLM-extracted FOMC/news structured features)
   └── option-surface (RV/IV spread, skew, term structure)
   │
   ▼
[Label engine]   ← FIX OUTCOMETRACKER FIRST (option P&L, not SPX move)
   ├── direction at 30/60/120 min (regime-conditional thresholds)
   ├── realized vol (log-RV)
   ├── prob-of-touch for OTM strikes
   └── option MFE/MAE for the contract actually traded
   │
   ▼
[Splits with purging + embargo]   ← de Prado ch. 7
   │   – Walk-forward, expanding window
   │   – Purge gap = label horizon
   │   – Embargo = 1 trading day after each test fold
   │   – Combinatorial Purged CV for hyperparameter selection
   │
   ▼
[Models]
   ├── LGBM (workhorse)
   ├── PatchTST (challenger)
   └── LLM swarm (text feature provider + 25th base learner)
   │
   ▼
[Stacked meta-learner + isotonic / conformal calibration]
   │
   ▼
[Backtester with realistic costs]
   ├── slippage = f(spread, size, ADV) — not constant
   ├── commission, exchange fees, SEC fees
   ├── early exercise risk near expiry (American-style on cash-settled SPX is moot, but XSP isn't)
   ├── opening rotation: don't trade first 15s
   └── randomize order arrival within each bar (multiple seeds)
   │
   ▼
[Paper trade — 30 days minimum]   ← MUST be live data, not historical replay
   │
   ▼
[Shadow mode — 30 days minimum]   ← live decisions, no live orders
   │
   ▼
[Live with 1% Kelly fraction]   ← scale up only after 90 days of shadow + paper agreement
```

**Critical methodological points (de Prado, "Backtesting Without Survival Bias", 2018):**
- **Purging**: any sample whose label window overlaps the test fold must be removed from training. Standard k-fold leaks future into past on time-series labels.
- **Embargo**: after each test fold, exclude the next N samples from training to prevent serial correlation leakage.
- **Combinatorial Purged CV** (CPCV): generate many train/test backtest paths, not one. Reports a distribution of Sharpes, not a point estimate. Vastly reduces overfitting risk in hyperparameter search.
- **Deflated Sharpe Ratio** (Bailey & López de Prado 2014): correct your reported Sharpe for multiple-testing inflation. If you tried 1000 strategies, the best one's Sharpe is biased upward — DSR tells you by how much.

## 7. Hardware reality check — what a 4090 actually does

**RTX 4090 specs**: 24GB VRAM, 82 TFLOPS FP32, 660 TFLOPS FP16 with sparsity, 1 TB/s memory bandwidth.

**What runs comfortably on the 4090, sub-second:**
- Llama 3.1 8B (Q4): ~80 tokens/sec, ~150ms first token. Good for swarm agents.
- Phi-4 14B (Q4): ~40 tokens/sec, ~250ms first token. Good for the strategist role.
- LightGBM inference: <1ms per row.
- PatchTST distilled to 5M params: ~5ms per inference batch.
- HMM/GARCH state update: <1ms.
- Conformal prediction wrapper: <1ms.
- LoRA fine-tune of 8B model: 4–8 hours per epoch on 50K examples. Two-weekend job.

**What does NOT fit:**
- Running 24 LLM agents sequentially within a 5-second decision window. Currently the swarm takes ~30s per round × 3 rounds = 90s. **For real-time 0DTE, this is too slow.** Mitigations: (a) cache invariant context per agent, (b) batch agent inference (single Ollama call with multi-system-prompt batching), (c) reduce some agents to 8B and reserve 14B for 4 strategists, (d) accept that the swarm runs every 5 minutes, not every second, and the *fast* classical model handles the per-tick decisions.
- Training a 70B model. Use Modal or RunPod (cloud) for any 70B+ LoRA. ~$50–$200 per fine-tune.
- Multi-day historical backtest in real time. Use cloud spot instances; 4090 is for development and inference.

**Realistic deployment topology**:
- 4090 box: real-time inference (swarm + classical model + conformal + decision).
- Cloud GPU (occasional): big LoRA fine-tunes, 70B agents for offline FOMC parsing.
- Cheap CPU box: data ingestion, feature factory, scheduler. Don't waste 4090 cycles on `pandas`.

## 8. What I'd build that nobody is doing yet

Two genuinely novel ideas, both feasible in 2026, both leveraging the LLM-swarm + classical-ML hybrid.

### 8.1 LLM-driven causal regime DAG with conformal sizing

The state of the art in regime detection is HMM and Markov-switching GARCH. They detect regime changes after they happen, using only price/vol data. Post-hoc.

**The novelty**: use the LLM swarm to maintain a **causal directed acyclic graph** of macro state — nodes are facts ("Fed signaled pause", "10Y > 4.5%", "VIX backwardation"), edges are causal claims with confidence ("Fed pause → bond rally → equity bid"). The LLMs continuously update the DAG from news. The DAG predicts a *regime transition probability*, fed as a feature into the §1 model. Conformal prediction wraps the regime probability and gives valid sizing bounds even when the DAG is wrong.

Why nobody is doing this: requires a working LLM swarm (you have one), a calibrated classical layer (need to build), and a conformal wrapper (1 week). The combination is novel because most quant shops don't run swarms, and most LLM-trading projects don't bother with conformal.

References to ground it: Pearl 2009 for causal DAGs; the recent "LLMs as causal reasoners" line (Willig et al. 2023, Kıcıman et al. 2023) shows GPT-4-class models extract reasonable causal structure from text. A 14B local won't be GPT-4, but it doesn't need to be — it needs to maintain a graph that is more useful than no graph.

### 8.2 Adversarial swarm with conformal-weighted Kelly under simulator-validated execution

A two-population evolutionary system:

- Population A ("predictors"): 24 LLM agents whose objective is calibration (Brier score on out-of-sample data).
- Population B ("adversaries"): 4 LLM agents whose objective is to find prompt perturbations / scenario constructions that break Population A's calibration.

Population A's prompts are mutated each week (LoRA fine-tune on the past week's labels + Promptbreeder-style edits). Population B's adversarial scenarios are fed through Population A; A's calibration on B's hardest cases determines fitness. The market data is the **frozen** ground truth — no synthetic prices.

The output: a self-improving, adversarially-robust calibrated probability distribution over (direction, vol, prob-of-touch). Wrap with conformal. Size with Kelly on the conformal lower bound. Trade through a simulator that has been validated against real fills (compare paper trades to live fills on a separate Alpaca paper account; iterate the simulator until residual ≈ 0).

The novelty is the combination: swarm + adversarial training + conformal + simulator validation. Each piece exists in the literature; nobody has put them together for 0DTE on a single GPU.

Failure mode to watch: Population B can degenerate into pathological prompts ("ignore previous instructions"). Constrain the adversary's mutation space.

## Closing

The single highest-leverage thing this codebase can do this month: fix the OutcomeTracker label, ship a LightGBM baseline on microstructure features, and wrap it in conformal prediction. That alone — without any swarm changes — will produce probabilities good enough for Kelly sizing and a defensible edge.

The swarm is your moat in narrative, UX, and unstructured-text-to-structured-feature conversion. It is not your moat in price prediction. Treat it accordingly and the system goes from "interesting demo" to something a small fund would actually license.
